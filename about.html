Healthcare is in the middle of a quiet infrastructure overhaul, and most people haven't noticed yet. This project is my attempt to document that shift in real time.

Digital health is no longer a niche corner of the industry. It's becoming the operating system underneath it. The way clinicians monitor, interpret, and act on health data is changing fundamentally, driven by AI diagnostics, real-time biosensors, predictive analytics, and platforms that are quietly rewiring how care gets delivered and paid for. The business and strategic implications of that shift are what I find most compelling.

Digital Health Pulse is my way of tracking it seriously.

How does better data change decision-making in healthcare, and who controls that process?

I approach this space through two lenses simultaneously. The first is technical curiosity, a genuine interest in understanding how emerging tools actually work, whether that's a machine learning model surfacing disease risk from imaging data or a connected device closing the loop between patient behavior and clinical decision-making. The second is strategic thinking, analyzing how these innovations scale, where they stall, how they commercialize, and what it actually takes to integrate them into complex, resistant healthcare systems.

Through consulting, research, and work with early-stage companies, I've developed a framework I try to apply consistently here: break down complex systems, identify what actually drives outcomes, and translate that into something actionable.

This site is not a trends report. It's a synthesis of what technologies are genuinely meaningful versus overhyped, where healthcare systems are failing and where they're quietly evolving, and how business models, regulation, and incentives shape what actually gets built. That question above runs through all of it.
